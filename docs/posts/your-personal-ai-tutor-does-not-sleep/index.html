Your Personal AI Tutor Does Not Sleep

ai education

I keep thinking about what it would have been like to grow up with an AI tutor. Not a search engine. Not a flashcard app. An actual intelligent interlocutor that knew what I understood, what I was confused about, and exactly how to explain the thing I was stuck on in a way that fit how my brain works.

That tutor does not exist yet in its ideal form, but the versions that exist now are already better than what most students have access to, and the trajectory is steep.

The fundamental problem with education is one-to-one instruction is expensive. A great teacher with twenty students cannot give each of them the individualized attention that would help them learn most effectively. Tutoring works because it is one-to-one. But private tutors are unaffordable for most families, which means individualized instruction has historically been a luxury good. AI tutors have the potential to collapse that inequality by making high-quality one-to-one instruction free and available on demand.

Khan Academy’s Khanmigo, which uses AI to tutor students through problems with guided questions rather than just giving answers, has shown genuinely promising results in early pilots. Students using Khanmigo show stronger learning gains than those using the standard platform. The AI is not replacing teachers. It is giving students something teachers do not have enough time to give: patient, individualized attention that meets them exactly where they are.

What fascinates me most is the adaptation aspect. A good human tutor learns how a student thinks over time and adjusts their approach. AI tutors do this too, but faster and with more data. An AI system can track not just whether a student got an answer right but what cognitive steps they took to get there, where they hesitated, what kinds of analogies helped them understand. That information is pedagogically invaluable, and it is being collected at a scale that no human researcher could achieve.

There are real concerns worth taking seriously. Screen time for children is already a significant concern, and an AI tutor that increases it is not obviously a net positive for wellbeing. The social dimension of education, learning from peers and developing collaborative skills, cannot be replicated by an AI tutor no matter how smart. And there is a risk that students become dependent on having an AI explain things rather than struggling through confusion, which can itself be pedagogically valuable.

None of these concerns are reasons to avoid the technology. They are reasons to deploy it thoughtfully. The students who are going to benefit most from AI tutors are the ones who currently have the least access to good education. That alone makes it worth taking seriously, even with the risks.
