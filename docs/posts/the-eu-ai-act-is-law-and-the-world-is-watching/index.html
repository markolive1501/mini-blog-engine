The EU AI Act Is Law, and the World Is Watching

ai regulation eu policy

Something genuinely historic happened in European regulatory history last year, and if you have been watching the US AI policy conversation you might have missed it. The EU AI Act became law. The first comprehensive AI regulatory framework from any major jurisdiction. Full stop.

Whether you think this is a triumph of precautionary governance or a innovation-killing bureaucratic overreach probably tells you more about your priors than about the regulation itself. But either way, it is in force now, and its effects will be global.

What The Act Actually Does

The EU AI Act uses a risk-based approach that classifies AI systems into four tiers. Unacceptable risk systems are simply banned. High risk systems face strict requirements. Limited risk systems have transparency obligations. Minimal risk systems are largely unregulated.

The banned category includes things that seem obviously wrong: social scoring systems by governments, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces with some exceptions, AI that manipulates people through subliminal techniques. These bans are the backbone of the framework and they came in earlier than many expected.

High risk systems, which include AI used in employment decisions, credit scoring, education assessment, and certain medical applications, face requirements around data quality, documentation, transparency, and human oversight. This is where the compliance burden lands most heavily.

Who It Actually Affects

If you are building AI products in the EU, this applies to you. If you are deploying AI products in the EU, this applies to you. The extraterritorial reach is surprisingly broad. Any system that interacts with people in the EU, or affects people in the EU, falls under the Act.

For US companies building AI products, this creates a compliance floor. Most sophisticated US companies building for the EU market are already building to these standards because EU market access is worth more than the compliance cost. The interesting question is whether this creates a de facto global standard for high-risk applications.

The Foundation Model Problem

The Act’s treatment of general-purpose AI models, often called foundation models, went through several contentious revisions. The final version requires providers of very capable foundation models to assess and document systemic risks, conduct adversarial testing, report incidents, and maintain cybersecurity measures.

The capability threshold for triggering these requirements is set at a computing level that only the most capable models currently reach. But the regulation includes a mechanism for updating that threshold as capabilities advance. That is smart regulatory design. It acknowledges that the capability landscape changes faster than legislation typically does.

What This Means Globally

Here is the thing about EU regulation of global technology platforms. GDPR created a de facto global standard for privacy because most large technology companies decided it was easier to apply GDPR globally than to maintain separate systems. The EU AI Act may do something similar for AI governance.

Whether that is good or bad depends on what you think about the specific rules, and I have views on that which I will save for another post. But the direction seems clear. The EU has moved first and moved comprehensively. The rest of the world is now reacting to that rather than setting the agenda.
