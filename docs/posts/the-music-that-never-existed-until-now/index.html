The Music That Never Existed Until Now

ai music knowledge

I spent twenty minutes last week generating songs with Suno. I typed things like “sad indie folk about moving to a new city” and “uptempo jazz with a female vocalist and piano solo” and in return I got complete songs with lyrics, melody, instrumentation, and production. Not demos. Not loops. Actual songs, three minutes long, with beginnings and endings and everything in between.

I was not sure how to feel about it, and I still am not.

Suno and its competitor Udio represent a genuine leap in AI music generation. Previous tools could produce convincing MIDI, rough loops, or stylized imitations of existing artists. These systems produce something fundamentally more finished. The quality is not at the level of a professional studio production, but it is not obviously worse than a lot of what you hear on independent radio. For certain genres, particularly electronic and pop-adjacent styles, the gap is remarkably small.

The creative implications are genuinely disorienting. A songwriter who wants to hear an arrangement of an idea no longer needs a studio, a band, or even a lot of musical skill. A podcaster can generate a custom theme tune without licensing anything. An independent game developer can have original music tailored to each level of their game. The production barrier that kept enormous amounts of creative vision trapped inside the heads of people who could not afford to record it has essentially collapsed.

But here is the part that makes musicians uncomfortable, and I think their discomfort is warranted. Suno and Udio are trained on existing music. The artists whose work was used to build these systems are not credited, compensated, or asked. A songwriter who has spent years developing their voice can now have that voice imitated by a model that learned from their catalogue without their knowledge or consent. That is a real harm, and the legal and ethical frameworks have not caught up with it at all.

The music industry is already in active litigation over these questions, and the answers will shape whether AI music generation becomes a tool that empowers artists or a replacement that devalues them. There are also interesting questions about what music becomes when anyone can generate anything. Scarcity used to give music value. If that scarcity is eliminated, the value shifts to things that are genuinely scarce: the human story behind the music, the live performance, the cultural moment that makes a song matter beyond its sonic qualities.

I am genuinely fascinated and slightly unsettled in equal measure. The technology is extraordinary. The ethics are murky. And the music itself, well, some of the songs Suno generated for me were genuinely pleasant to listen to. That is probably the most unsettling part of all.
