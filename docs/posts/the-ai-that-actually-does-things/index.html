The AI That Actually Does Things

ai agents

There is a quiet revolution happening in AI right now, and it does not involve better chatbot responses. It involves AI that stops talking and starts doing.

The distinction matters more than it sounds. A chatbot is a glorified autocomplete machine. You ask it something, it generates an answer, and the interaction ends. Whether that answer actually solves your problem is your problem. But an AI agent works differently. It takes a goal, breaks it into steps, uses tools, adapts when things go wrong, and does not stop until something real is finished. That is a fundamentally different relationship with a machine.

Think of it as the difference between hiring someone who answers emails and hiring someone who runs your social media. Both are technically interacting with the same platforms, but one needs hand-holding at every step while the other just gets on with it.

The agentic AI wave started creeping into mainstream products around late 2024. Anthropic built Claude into tools that can browse the web, write and run code, and hand off tasks between specialized sub-agents. OpenAI gave ChatGPT memory and the ability to use plugins that let it take actions in the real world. Microsoft rolled out Copilot agents that can draft emails, analyze data, and schedule meetings autonomously. None of these are perfect, but they represent something genuinely new: AI that has feet.

What makes agents so interesting is not any single capability. It is the combination. A good agent needs to understand what you want, figure out how to get there, execute multiple steps, recover from errors, and know when to ask for help rather than blindly pressing on. That is a coordination problem, and it turns out AI is getting surprisingly good at coordination.

The real test is in software development. Writing a single function is table stakes for modern AI. But an agent that can read a codebase, understand what a new feature should do, plan the implementation across multiple files, write the code, run the tests, and report back with results? That is something else entirely. Tools like Devin, Claude’s agentic coding features, and emerging products from startups like Augment Code are pointing in that direction. Some are still rough, but the trajectory is clear.

Of course, the moment you hand a system the keys to take actions on your behalf, you also hand it the ability to take wrong actions on your behalf. Agentic AI introduces a new category of risk: not just a bad answer, but a bad outcome compounded across multiple steps. If a chatbot hallucinates, you notice. If an agent hallucinates a plan, it might execute that plan confidently and create a mess you did not ask for. Safety and oversight are not optional add-ons here. They are load-bearing walls.

Despite the risks, the momentum is undeniable. The companies building the best agents are not just competing on benchmark scores anymore. They are competing on reliability, persistence, and the ability to actually complete things. That is a more boring metric than a viral demo, but it is also the one that matters when AI graduates from toy to tool.

The age of AI that just talks is ending. The age of AI that acts is just beginning.
