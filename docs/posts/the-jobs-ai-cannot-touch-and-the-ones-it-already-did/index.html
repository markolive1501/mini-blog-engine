The Jobs AI Cannot Touch, and the Ones It Already Did

ai jobs economy

Every technology wave brings the same conversation. This time is different, people say, and then it is not different in the ways they expected. The printing press made scribes obsolete but created more jobs than it destroyed overall. The ATM machine was supposed to kill bank tellers, and the number of bank tellers actually grew after ATMs became widespread. Optimists and pessimists have been having this argument for two centuries, and the optimists have a pretty good track record.

I am not sure this time maps cleanly onto those historical patterns, and I think the people saying that are underestimating the scope of what is happening.

Generative AI is not automating specific physical tasks or specialized data processing. It is automating cognitive labor across domains simultaneously. Legal associates summarizing documents. Radiologists reading scans. Translators. Copywriters. Paralegals. Junior analysts at investment banks. These are not peripheral roles. They are the entry points into professions, the jobs that young professionals spend years training for and that form the economic backbone of the knowledge economy. When those roles start shrinking, the pipeline into those careers changes in ways that are hard to reverse.

What is genuinely new is the speed. Previous automation waves happened over decades. This one is happening over years. A technology that did not exist in any meaningful commercial form in 2022 was processing real work at scale by 2024. That pace does not give institutions, governments, or individuals time to adapt smoothly.

The most resilient roles share some common characteristics. Jobs that require genuine physical presence in unpredictable environments, like plumbers, electricians, and other tradespeople, are relatively insulated because robotics has not caught up to the dexterity and adaptability required. Roles requiring deep personal relationships, high-stakes emotional judgment, or truly novel problem-solving are also harder to automate because the training data for those qualities does not really exist in a form AI can replicate.

But resilience is not the same as immunity. A nurse who spends half their time documenting patient information is far more exposed than a nurse who spends that time at the bedside. The administrative layer of almost every profession is shrinking, and the people in those roles are discovering that the skills they spent years developing are the ones being automated first.

The honest answer is that no one knows how this ends. History suggests new categories of work emerge that we cannot currently predict. It also suggests that the transition can be brutal for individuals even when the aggregate economic effect is positive. Those two facts do not cancel each other out. We need to hold them both at once.
