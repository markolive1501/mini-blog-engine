WordPress powers 43% of the web. Twenty billion page views a month. It's not just a website builder — it's *the* internet in many people's eyes. And as of Friday, it officially lets AI agents write, edit, and publish posts on your behalf. All via natural language commands.

The feature set is broad: draft, edit, manage comments, update metadata, organize content with tags and categories. If you've ever used WordPress, you know how tedious some of that housekeeping is. Automating it through a chat prompt sounds genuinely useful.

But here's the part worth sitting with: **posts authored by AI go to draft by default.** They don't go live automatically. A human has to approve them first.

That approval gate is doing a lot of narrative work. WordPress isn't saying "AI will replace your writers." It's saying "AI will help your writers — but the last word is still yours." That's a very deliberate message. And it probably reflects something real about user sentiment right now.

## So What's Actually Happening?

The mechanics are straightforward. You link your WordPress site to an AI agent, give it credentials, and then prompt it like a very fast, very literal assistant. "Write a post about our new product launch." The agent drafts it, saves it as a draft, and you review it before it appears on your site.

The question isn't really whether the technology works. It probably does, at least for routine content. The question is whether *you* — the site owner, the brand manager, the blogger — actually want this sitting in your workflow.

For solo bloggers or small businesses without a dedicated content team, this could be a genuine productivity unlock. For anyone with a strong voice or a discerning audience, it's more complicated. AI drafts tend to sound like AI drafts. The approval step might become a heavier lift than just writing the thing yourself.

## The Bigger Picture

What makes this significant isn't the feature itself — it's the scale. When a platform serving 43% of all websites decides AI publishing is a priority, it normalizes something. It signals to millions of site owners that AI content creation is now a mainstream expectation, not a fringe experiment.

That's a bet on where the internet is going: more content, faster turnaround, humans in the supervisory role. Whether that's good for the web is a separate question. But WordPress has made its bet.

The approval gate, though. Keep an eye on that. It's the bit that suggests WordPress knows something about its own users. The future of AI publishing might not be fully automated — it might just be humans getting very fast first drafts.
