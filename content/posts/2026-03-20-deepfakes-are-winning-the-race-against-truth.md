I want to be direct about something that I think is not being discussed with enough urgency.

In the 2024 election cycle, AI-generated deepfake videos of candidates saying things they never said were viewed millions of times before being fact-checked. In 2025, a Hong Kong firm was defrauded of $25 million when an AI-generated video call convinced finance staff they were taking instructions from the CFO. In early 2026, deepfake nude images of minors generated from school photos became a criminal justice problem in multiple countries simultaneously.

This is not a future problem. This is a present problem. And the gap between our ability to generate synthetic media and our ability to detect and respond to it is currently wide and closing slowly.

## The Economics Are Brutal

Making a convincing deepfake used to require significant technical expertise, expensive hardware, and time. Today it requires a photo and a subscription to a service that costs less than a Netflix subscription. The quality threshold for causing real-world harm is much lower than the quality threshold for impressing people on social media.

Most harmful deepfakes do not need to be perfect. They need to be plausible enough to share before people check, to spread faster than corrections can travel. The information ecosystem is structured in a way that advantages the first mover, and synthetic media is a first mover's paradise.

## Detection Is Losing

I want to be careful here because there are genuinely talented researchers working on detection methods, and progress is being made. The problem is the asymmetry. Generating a deepfake requires compute that is getting cheaper. Detecting a deepfake requires compute that must stay ahead of generation, which means it must always be harder and more expensive than the generation side.

Every time a detection method becomes reliable, someone finds a way to defeat it. The adversarial dynamic is not new, but the speed and accessibility of the attack side is unprecedented.

## The Consent Problem Nobody Is Solving

Beyond the immediate fraud and political manipulation, there is a consent problem that is not being adequately addressed. Deepfake technology can take a single photograph and generate video of that person in situations they were never in, saying things they never said, in contexts that may be pornographic, defamatory, or simply contrary to their values.

The legal frameworks for this are fragmented and slow. In most jurisdictions, creating a deepfake of someone without consent is not yet clearly illegal in all contexts. The enforcement mechanisms are almost non-existent. The speed of the technology is outpacing the speed of every other relevant system we have.

## What We Should Actually Be Worried About

My genuine concern is not any single deepfake. It is the aggregate effect of a media environment where nothing can be trusted by default. When anything can be synthetic, the logical response is to trust nothing. That is a quiet, creeping effect on public discourse that happens gradually and then suddenly.

We are already seeing early signs. Journalists are struggling with source verification. Courts are dealing with synthetic evidence. The concept of documentary truth is under genuine stress. These are not abstract concerns. They are structural changes to how information systems work.

The deepfake problem is solvable in the same way climate change is solvable. The technology exists or can be developed. The question is whether we coordinate fast enough to matter.
