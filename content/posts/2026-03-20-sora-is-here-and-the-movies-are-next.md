Let me tell you something that still weirds me out when I think about it too long.

Two years ago, AI generating video from text was a parlor trick. A few seconds of blurry, obviously fake footage that fell apart the moment anything moved. Today, OpenAI's Sora can produce minutes of coherent, visually rich video from a single prompt. Not perfect video. Not film-ready video. But video that, in certain contexts, already passes a casual human eye.

That is a cliff edge, not a slope.

## What Sora Actually Does Now

Sora generates up to 20 seconds of 1080p video from text prompts. It understands physics in a loose, statistical way. It can handle multiple characters, camera movements, lighting changes, and fairly complex scene descriptions. The coherence is the shock. Early video models fell apart because they could not hold a scene together across frames. Sora mostly can.

The remaining gaps are telling. Fine text dissolves into gibberish. Cause and effect occasionally confuses itself. Sustained physics simulation still fails. But these are engineering problems with known paths to solutions, not fundamental limitations.

## The Creative Industries Are Already Responding

What is interesting is watching different industries process this at different speeds. Independent creators are moving fast, using AI video for B-roll, concept visualization, and experimental short-form content. The mainstream film industry is more cautious, partly for quality reasons and partly because the labour implications are genuinely disruptive.

Hollywood spent decades fighting technological disruption. VCRs, digital effects, streaming. Each time the pattern was the same: initial panic, eventual integration, changed but surviving industry. AI video probably follows a similar arc, but the speed is faster and the scope is wider.

## The Deep Fake Problem Is Already Here

I want to be direct about this because too much of the AI video conversation is framed around creative possibilities and not enough around misuse. Sora, and tools like it from Runway, Pika, and others, make it trivially easy to generate convincing footage of anyone saying or doing anything. Not perfectly convincing to a forensic eye, but convincing enough for social media, for WhatsApp forwards, for the context where most people encounter video.

We are already seeing this in political content, in harassment campaigns, in fraud. The tooling is outpacing the detection capability, and that gap is currently wide.

## What Comes Next

The trajectory is clear. Minutes become longer. Quality improves. Real footage and synthetic footage become harder to distinguish without specialized tools. The question is not whether this technology matures, it clearly will. The question is whether the social infrastructure, the detection tools, the legal frameworks, and the public literacy catch up in time.

My guess is partially yes and partially no. We will get better at detection. We will build some infrastructure. And we will also have a genuinely chaotic period where synthetic video is a serious information problem before any of that stabilises.

Sora is the beginning of that period, not the end of it.
