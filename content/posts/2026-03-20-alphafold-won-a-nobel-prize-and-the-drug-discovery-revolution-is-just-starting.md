In 2020, DeepMind's AlphaFold solved what scientists had called the folding problem, a challenge so fiendishly difficult that it had occupied structural biologists for fifty years. In 2024, Demis Hassabis and John Jumper won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for this work. By any measure, this is a extraordinary sequence of events for a piece of software.

But here is the thing that strikes me most about AlphaFold. The Nobel Prize is not the climax of this story. It is barely the opening act.

## What AlphaFold Actually Does

Proteins are the machinery of life. Their function is determined almost entirely by their three-dimensional structure, which is itself determined by the sequence of amino acids that compose them. Predicting that structure from the amino acid sequence alone is the protein folding problem, and it matters because understanding structure means understanding function.

Before AlphaFold, determining a single protein structure could take years of painstaking experimental work. AlphaFold does it in minutes, with accuracy that in many cases matches experimental techniques. That is not a marginal improvement. It is a phase transition in what's possible.

## Why Drug Discovery Changes Completely

Most drugs work by binding to specific proteins and modifying their behaviour. If you know the structure of the target protein, you can use computational methods to search for molecules that bind effectively. Without that structure, you are essentially working blind.

AlphaFold gave researchers around the world access to structures that previously would have taken years to determine. The number of known protein structures has expanded from thousands to hundreds of millions in just a few years. That expansion is already accelerating drug discovery pipelines.

## The Real Revolution Is Still Ahead

The current limitations are revealing. AlphaFold predicts static structures well but struggles with proteins that are flexible, that change shape significantly when performing their biological function, or that interact with other molecules in complex ways. The dynamics of protein behaviour are still an active research frontier.

More importantly, predicting a structure is not the same as predicting how a drug will behave in a complex biological system. The gap between in-silico prediction and clinical success remains significant. We are getting better at closing that gap, but it is not closed yet.

## What This Means For Medicine

The honest expectation is something like this: over the next decade, the combination of AlphaFold-derived structural data, better molecular dynamics simulation, and AI-assisted drug design will compress the early stages of drug discovery significantly. Years of wet lab work will be replaced by months of computational screening followed by much more targeted experimental validation.

This does not mean drugs appear overnight. The clinical trial process exists for good reasons and will not disappear. But the bottleneck shifts from discovery to validation, which is a fundamentally different and more tractable problem.

AlphaFold is a genuine landmark. The Nobel Prize is deserved. And the work it enabled is only just getting started.
