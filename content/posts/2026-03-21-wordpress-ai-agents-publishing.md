Last Friday, WordPress.com quietly made a bet on what the internet looks like in 2027.

The platform — which powers over 43% of all websites and sees roughly 20 billion page views every month — announced it will now let AI agents draft, edit, and publish content on customers' sites. Not just suggest edits. *Publish*. The catch: by default, AI-authored posts land in your drafts folder, waiting for a human to hit approve. But even with that guard rail, the implication is hard to miss.

This is a publishing platform that decided the future looks less like a human at a keyboard and more like a human with a keyboard *supervising* a keyboard.

Here's how it works. You go to wordpress.com/mcp, toggle on the capabilities you want, connect your preferred AI client — Claude, Cursor, ChatGPT, whatever MCP-enabled tool you're running — and then describe what you want published in plain English. The agent reads your theme, understands your colors and layout, and generates a post. It can also manage comments, restructure categories, fix alt text, and reorganize your taxonomy.

That's a lot of territory covered for "I told it what I wanted."

## Why this matters more than a typical feature drop

WordPress isn't a scrappy startup running an experiment. It's infrastructure. When it moves, the web moves with it.

The company has been building toward this for a while. Last fall it added MCP support — the Model Context Protocol that lets AI assistants tap into a site's content, settings, and analytics. Now it's graduating to write access. The gap between "AI can read my site" and "AI can publish on my site" just closed, and that's a meaningful line crossed.

There are obvious upsides. A small business owner who dreads writing product descriptions now has a capable first-draft machine. A blogger who struggles with consistency can keep a posting schedule without burning out. The barrier to maintaining a decent-looking website just got lower.

But the honest version of this story includes the uncomfortable parts too.

## The approval problem

Here is the guard rail: posts go to draft. Human approval required.

That's reassuring in principle. In practice, it's worth asking: how many people will actually read what the AI wrote before clicking publish? And if the answer is "not many," the guard rail becomes a formality rather than a safeguard.

The web already has a content quality problem. SEO-driven slop, auto-generated product descriptions, thin affiliate posts that exist only to rank — and that's before AI was in the loop. WordPress.com's new capabilities might turbocharge all of that. The same tools that help a diligent blogger post more often will also help a spammer scale up.

## The interesting question underneath

What WordPress is really doing is testing a hypothesis: do people *want* to be out of the loop on their own content?

For a certain kind of site owner — the kind who thinks of publishing as a chore rather than a craft — the answer might genuinely be yes. And that's fine. Not every post needs to be a considered piece of writing.

But the more interesting question isn't whether AI *can* write for you. It's whether you can tell the difference when it does — and whether it matters if you can't.

The machine is writing now. Your job is to decide how much you care about that.
