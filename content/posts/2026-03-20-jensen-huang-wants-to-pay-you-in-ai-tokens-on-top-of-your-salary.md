Jensen Huang has a new benefits package in mind, and it does not include health insurance.

At a recent NVIDIA event, the CEO floated an idea that sounds like a startup perk pitch but is actually a fairly deliberate bet on what work looks like in five to ten years: employees would receive AI tokens, on top of their regular salary, as a kind of performance dividend paid in compute. Those tokens represent access to AI agent time, and employees could deploy their agents, sell them, or gift them to colleagues.

On its face, it reads like a clever retention mechanism wrapped in futuristic packaging. But the deeper logic is revealing. Huang has been telling anyone who will listen that NVIDIA's GPUs are not just selling to companies, they are selling to a coming infrastructure layer where every knowledge worker has a team of AI agents working alongside them. If that future arrives, the tokens employees receive become a stake in the very compute economy NVIDIA is building.

## The 100 agents per employee vision

NVIDIA has also been explicit about the scale it expects. Within a decade, the company reportedly believes each employee at NVIDIA could have 100 AI agents working under their direction. That is not a staffing plan, it is an operating model. And it is a vision the entire industry seems to be quietly adopting: Microsoft, Google, Salesforce, and a dozen well-funded startups are all racing to build the agent infrastructure that makes this plausible.

The token concept is interesting because it solves a real problem. If your AI agents generate value, who owns that value? A salary is fixed. Equity only works for public companies or startups. But a token, paid by your employer, that you can deploy, trade, or convert, creates a direct alignment between the work your agents do and your personal compensation. It is, in a sense, extending performance pay into the agent layer.

## What this signals about the AI industry

Here is the uncomfortable part. NVIDIA's vision only works if AI agents actually become useful enough, and cheap enough, that deploying 100 of them per employee makes economic sense. That is a significant assumption. Today's agents can draft emails, summarise meetings, and run basic research tasks. They fall apart on anything that requires sustained judgment, cross-domain reasoning, or tolerance for ambiguity.

The gap between "useful demo" and "100 agents per employee" is not a feature list, it is a research problem. And the companies betting hardest on agent proliferation, NVIDIA prominently included, are also the ones most invested in the compute costs staying high enough, and demand staying voracious enough, to justify continued GPU spending at the scale they are projecting.

Huang is right that the workforce is changing. The question is whether his vision of tokenised AI labour is a genuine preview of how compensation evolves, or a very well-pitched story designed to keep the GPU orders flowing. Possibly both.

The token pay idea has not caught on anywhere else yet, and for good reason: it is hard to govern, hard to tax, and hard to explain on a pay slip. But ideas that sound strange in March often look obvious by September. Watch this space.
