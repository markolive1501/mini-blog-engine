Here is a number that should make you feel both uneasy and hopeful: studies have repeatedly found that AI diagnostic systems can detect certain cancers in medical imaging with an accuracy rate that matches or exceeds that of experienced radiologists. Not close. Not almost. Actually matches or beats them, depending on the study and the condition.

Radiology is arguably the medical field most exposed to AI disruption right now, and for a specific reason. Reading scans is pattern recognition at scale. A radiologist spends years training their eye to spot subtle shadows, asymmetries, and textures that indicate disease. AI systems, trained on millions of labelled images, learn the same patterns from the other direction, and they do not get tired, distracted, or have off days. They also do not forget what they learned.

Google Health's deep learning system for breast cancer screening has shown lower false positive rates than human radiologists in multiple trials. AI tools for detecting diabetic retinopathy from eye scans have received regulatory approval in multiple countries. Systems for identifying stroke indicators in CT scans can alert doctors to critical cases faster than the normal triage process, potentially saving brain tissue in the process. These are not laboratory curiosities. They are deployed systems saving lives.

What makes this genuinely exciting beyond the raw numbers is the democratization effect. A rural hospital in a country with few specialist radiologists can now run imaging through an AI system that has effectively seen more cases than any individual doctor could accumulate in a lifetime of practice. The knowledge is not distributed unequally anymore. It is embedded in the software and accessible anywhere there is a computer.

The challenges are real though. Medical AI systems can fail in ways that are different from human failure. They can be confidently wrong in cases that are unusual or outside their training distribution. They can propagate biases present in historical medical data, since healthcare data reflects existing inequalities in who gets treated and how. Regulatory approval for medical AI is also genuinely difficult, and the pace of regulation often struggles to keep up with the pace of model improvement.

There is also the question no one talks about enough: what happens to the doctors. Radiologists are not going to disappear overnight, but their role is shifting. They are becoming supervisors of AI systems rather than primary readers of every scan. That is a different job, and the transition will be awkward. The best radiologists I have spoken to seem to understand this and are adapting by learning to work with the tools rather than against them.

The net effect looks positive to me, and not just in the abstract. If an AI system catches a cancer that would have been missed, the patient does not care whether it came from an algorithm or a doctor. What matters is that it was caught. Medicine has always been a collaboration between human judgment and whatever tools are available. AI is just the most capable tool we have ever had.
