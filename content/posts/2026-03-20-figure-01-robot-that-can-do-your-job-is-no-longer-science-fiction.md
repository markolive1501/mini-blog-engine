There is a moment in every technological transition where the thing that seemed like a 20-year-away problem suddenly is not a 20-year-away problem anymore. Figure 01 feels like that moment for humanoid robotics.

Figure, the robotics company backed by Microsoft, OpenAI, and NVIDIA, has been making consistent and frankly unsettling progress. Their humanoid robot, a roughly human-sized machine called Figure 01, can now hold coherent conversations, interpret visual input, perform complex manipulation tasks in real environments, and do this not in a lab but in actual BMW manufacturing facilities.

That sentence should land differently than it does. We have had robots in factories for decades. What we have not had is robots that can learn new tasks, understand natural language instructions, and adapt to novel situations without months of engineering per task.

## Why Humanoid Shape Matters

The choice to build humanoid robots is deliberate. The world is designed for human bodies. Steps, doorways, tools, shelves, workstations, all built around human kinematics. A humanoid robot can theoretically do anything a human can do in a physical space without requiring the space itself to be redesigned.

This is an enormously pragmatic bet. Instead of rebuilding the world for robots, Figure is building robots that can navigate the world as it exists. The implications for labour markets are immediate. The implications for everything else are strange and far-reaching.

## What Figure 01 Can Actually Do

The current capabilities are impressive and also, honestly, a bit limited in ways that matter. Figure 01 can make coffee, sort objects, follow verbal instructions to perform multi-step tasks, and carry on basic conversations about what it is doing. In controlled environments with well-defined tasks, it performs reliably.

The hard problems are the messy ones. Unpredictable environments, ambiguous instructions, physical edge cases. The gap between lab performance and real-world robustness is still real. But it is shrinking faster than most people in the robotics field expected even three years ago.

## The Labour Question

I keep coming back to the labour implications because they are so obviously massive and yet so under-discussed in mainstream coverage. A humanoid robot that can be trained on new tasks in weeks rather than months, that works 24 hours without breaks, that does not require healthcare or pension contributions, that can be deployed in existing factory floors without modification.

Figure is careful to frame this as augmenting human workers rather than replacing them. BMW's involvement suggests a more nuanced deployment story than wholesale replacement. But the direction of travel is not subtle.

## Where This Goes

The honest answer is nobody knows for certain, but the pattern is clear. Physical capability is following a similar curve to language capability a few years ago. Impressive demos become real products become mass-deployed infrastructure. The timeline is probably longer than the most aggressive proponents claim, but shorter than the sceptics expect.

Figure 01 is not the robot from Ex Machina. It is not even close. But it is a serious piece of hardware doing serious work in the real world. The question is not whether the physical world gets integrated into AI systems. It clearly will. The question is how fast, and who benefits.
